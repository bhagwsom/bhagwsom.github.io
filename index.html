About Me.

Hi, I am Somya! I am a student at the School of Information at the University of Michigan working toward a Bachelor of Science in Information (BSI). I am minoring in Science, Technology, and Society from the Residential College in the College of Literature, Science, and Arts because I am intrigued by the dynamic interaction between technology, information, and cultures.                

I am interested in UI/UX, digital marketing, information security, and data analysis. I have honed my technical skills in coding and statistics with my coursework at the School of Information and nurtured my cultural competence with Spanish communication coursework in addition to my blended South Asian-American ethnic background.

Maturing as a global citizen by exposure to diverse friends from schools in the city of Saginaw, MI to the University of Michigan, I see the world enriched by our unique cultural backgrounds. However, I also see the risk of a cultural divide through filter bubbles, echo chambers, miscommunications, and inequitable access to information. My goal is to be part of the solution by increasing understanding among people by using the human connection.        

When not working, I love to travel, explore new places and create memories through photography. More than commemorating landmarks, I like to take my lens to local lives to capture their struggles and stories. Growing up in Saginaw, it surprised me that most friends my age were either oblivious to or just overlooked the economic and racial disparities which drew my attention. I dwelled on the disparate lives of people and realized then that success in business lies not just in making profit but in enhancing lives of people. I hope to create viable ways to connect all people with information technology and be at the forefront of what I see as a ‘techno-humanistic’ approach to business.  

Always aiming to expand my limits, I also focus on personal goals such as fitness and meditation. I like to live my life like Walt Whitman’s quote “I believe that a leaf of grass is no less than the journey-work of the stars.” I aim to bring a more humanistic approach to Information and be a part of this new business era- an era in which business is poised to acquire a soul.

CME Group GIS Project

I interned for CME Group on the Global Information Security Team in Summer 2019 where I worked closely with my manager Ivan Clatanoff, and my mentors Josephine Romero and Huyen Vo to create a database application to streamline annual planning using Apex. This application allowed users to input their desired future projects and estimated time/resources to calculate feasibility based on the threshold of profees, capital, maintenance, and RTB/Project hours for the year. During my 12 week internship, I was able to learn Apex, create, user-test, and design this application. It is currently in use within the Global Information Security team to help allocate resources, provide a workspace for potential deeper resource allocation, and calculating project feasibility. Information Flow Case Study

The Dabbawallas of Mumbai are a group of people that take part in a lunch box delivery system throughout the city. A ‘dabba’ is a lunch container and a ‘wala’ is a mobile street vendor. These dabbawallas have been coordinating the delivery of homecooked meals to thousands of Indians for hundreds of years. They carry tiffins of food from the home to the office of the worker and then the empty tiffins from the office back to the home in an organized fashion. This paper analyzes the organizational structures within the Dabbawallas of Mumbai who have harnessed the power of code in a non-traditional format. This information flow has brought success for hundreds of years due to the infrastructure's simplicity and control.

Into the Black Box: Twitter Edition

Since the 2016 Cambridge Analytica Scandal, users are becoming more aware that their online interactions have consequences beyond what they originally thought. Now, consequences can shape or influence users’ free thinking. Social media sites seem to be black boxes where very few people completely understand the extent of how much data is collected and harvested about a user. With Twitter, users are not aware of just how much information it collects about the user, apart from the voluntary information given by the user in tweets and interactions with the page. This paper hopes to analyze the extent to which Twitter collects data about a person beyond their voluntary information. We consider voluntary information to be what the user has tweeted, liked, retweeted, or any other actions on the page along with the information they have chosen to provide to Twitter such as their name, birthdate, etc.

Storytelling.

I learned the power of stories at a young age. I consider myself a mental world traveler in that I do not need a plane ticket to explore. I learned that I could have intriguing experiences simply by engaging in deep conversation. Through stories, I have vicariously been everywhere- from bicycling through France selling flowers to touring houses in Detroit selling real-estate.

As people hear stories, they gain new perspectives and can participate as global citizens. They can escape their echo chambers to commiserate with Syrian refugees and #MeToo marchers as well as rejoice with victims saved through miraculous relief efforts. People can live vicariously through others to understand their own unfathomable struggles often untouched by media..

Students of Umich

Personal Project- Founder|Photographer|Storyteller
September 2016- Present

At U of M, inspired by Brandon Stanton’s blogging methods in Humans of New York, I brought my love for storytelling and photography to create a blog called Students of Umich. It uses Instagram and Facebook to connect to students’ journeys as discuss raw, uncensored and taboo topics -- from surviving sexual assault to eating disorders. I wanted this to be an account where people escape their echo chambers.

Starting this account as a freshman, I solely manage, run, and have built the account to over 4,500 followers.
